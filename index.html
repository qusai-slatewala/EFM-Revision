Introduction to Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics studies the behavior of the whole economy (national and international). It originated after the Great Depression of 1929 when classical theory (which assumed there would always be full employment) failed to explain massive unemployment (25% in the USA). Macroeconomics was considered to be a separate part of economics after the publishing of General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money in 1936 by the British economist, John Maynard Keynes.

Classical Approach: Before Keynes, the classical approach believed there would always be full employment as long as there were workers who were willing to work and factories that were ready to function at their full capacity. This theory did not hold well during the Great Depression when there were large numbers of job cuts in North America and Europe.

Primary Goal: To maintain stability in the general price level and resolve the major macro problems of the economy such as inflation and unemployment.

Scope of Macroeconomics: Economic Growth and Development, Theory of National Income, Theory of Money, Theory of International Trade, Theory of Employment, Theory of General Price Level, Fiscal Policy, and Monetary Policy.

Circular Flow in a Four-Sector Economy

The economy consists of four interacting sectors through which money and resources flow:

1. Household Sector

Provides factors of production (land, labour, capital and enterprise). The household sectors are the consumers who consume the goods and services produced by the firms and make return payments.

2. Firms Sector

Includes all the business entities, corporations and partnerships. The primary function of this sector is to produce goods and services for sale in the market and make factor payments to the household sector.

3. Government Sector

Includes the center, state, and local governments. The prime function of this sector is to regulate the functioning of the economy. Incurs both revenue and capital expenditure.

4. Foreign Sector

Handles transactions with the rest of the world. Foreign trade implies net exports (exports minus imports). Exports are goods sold to the rest of the world; imports are goods produced abroad and sold domestically.

Leakages vs. Injections

  • Leakages: An outflow of income from the circular flow model that is not used to purchase goods and services. The three main leakages are savings, taxes and imports.
  • Injections: An inflow of income to the circular flow. The volume of income increases due to this. The three main injections are investment, government expenditure, and exports.
  • Equilibrium condition: In a circular flow, leakages = injections.

National Income Concepts

National income is the total monetary value of all final services and goods that are produced by a nation during a period of time. It is the net result of all economic activities of any country during a period of one year, calculated in terms of money. Formula: Y = C + I + G + (X - M).

  • To compute and compare the economic advancement of a country.
  • To measure the contribution of different sectors to the country's economic growth.
  • To assist the government in planning and implementing various projects.
  • To determine the limitations and benefits of various economic activities like production, consumption, and distribution.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP): The standard measure of the value added created through the production of goods and services in a country. Excludes intermediate goods to avoid double counting. Calculated at a stable base year price to compute actual value.

Net National Product (NNP): The market value of all finished goods produced by citizens (domestic and overseas) minus depreciation. NNP = GNP - Depreciation.

Importance of NNP: It serves as a major determinant of a country's actual growth rate and strength. By comparing a country's NNP to another country's, the growth status of the country is gauged.

Components of National Income (Y = C + I + G + (X - M)):
C = private consumption, I = gross private investment, G = government spending, X = exports, M = imports.

  1. Income Method: Measured at distribution level as a flow of factor incomes.
    Formula: Employee compensation + Operating surplus (wages + rent + profit + mixed income) + Net factor income from abroad.
  2. Product/Value Added Method: Measured at production level as a flow of goods and services.
    Formula: Gross national product - Cost of Capital - Depreciation - Indirect Taxes.
  3. Expenditure Method: Measured at spending level. Must ensure spending is only counted once.
    Formula: Consumer spending + Net Govt Expenditure + Capital Spending (Investment) + Net Exports.
  • Personal Income (PI): All earnings made by a household (salary, wages, dividends, rent). Equation: Private income - Corporate profit tax - Undistributed profits by corporations + Transfers by corporations and government to households.
  • Disposable Income (DPI): Money available for household consumption, savings, and spending after income taxes are deducted (DPI = PI - Personal Income Taxes).
  • Discretionary Income: The amount of net income remaining after all necessities (shelter, food, debts) are covered.
  • Per Capita Income (PCI): Average income earned per person (National Income / Population). Includes all individuals, not just working age. Used to evaluate standard of living.

Journey of the Indian Economy

India adopted a mixed economy model post-independence (1947) to counter the impoverishment inflicted by British deindustrialization. Inspired by command economies like the Soviet Union, India turned to comprehensive economic planning.

  • 1st Five-Year Plan (1951): Harrod-Domar model focused on agriculture and irrigation. Achieved annualized growth of 3.6%.
  • 2nd Five-Year Plan (1956): Mahalanobis model focused on rapid heavy industrialization (Licence Raj era).
  • Lal Bahadur Shastri: Focus on food security, initiation of the Green Revolution (high-yield seeds), MSP regime introduction, and Operation Flood (milk).
  • The Hindu Rate of Growth: Early bureaucratic systems and Licence Raj limited innovation, resulting in a slow economic growth rate of around 3.5%.
  • Indira Gandhi: 'Garibi Hatao' slogan (1971). Nationalization of 14 major private banks in 1969. FERA (1973) enacted to control foreign investments.
  • 1980s (Rajiv Gandhi): Early deregulation, reduction in import duties, and ushering in the IT & telecom revolutions. Establishment of NABARD (1982).
  • 1991 Crisis: High fiscal deficit (8.4% of GDP), low forex reserves (enough for only 2 weeks of imports), high double-digit inflation. Crude oil prices surged due to the Gulf War.
  • Reforms (Narasimha Rao & Manmohan Singh): Liberalisation (ending Licence Raj), Privatisation (disinvestment), and Globalisation (unrestricted cross-border movement). Abolished quantitative restrictions on imports.

Business Cycle, Unemployment, and Inflation

The Business Cycle

Types of Unemployment

Frictional

Short-term; transitioning between jobs or looking for new employment.

Structural

Longer-lasting; caused by fundamental technological shifts in the economy.

Cyclical

Resulting directly from economic downturns in the business cycle.

Disguised

When workers are redundant; their marginal productivity is essentially zero (common in agriculture).

Inflation

The general, sustained increase in prices, resulting in a decrease in the purchasing power of money. According to Coulborn, inflation is defined as "too much money chasing too few goods."

  • Causes: Money Supply Increases, Money Demand Decreases, Aggregate Demand Increases (Demand-Pull), Aggregate Supply Decreases (Cost-Push).
  • Types based on rate: Creeping (<3%), Walking (3-10%), Running (>10%), Galloping (20-1000%), Hyperinflation (>1000%).
  • Stagflation: High inflation + Economic stagnation.

Multiplier, Trade & Exchange Rates

Developed by Keynes during the Great Depression. It explains how an initial change in spending leads to a more than proportional change in national income. One person's spending becomes another's income.

Formula: k = 1 / (1 - MPC). A higher Marginal Propensity to Consume leads to a larger multiplier effect.

  • Fixed (Pegged): Currency tied to another major currency (e.g., USD).
  • Flexible (Floating): Value determined by market forces (supply/demand).
  • Intermediate (Hybrid): E.g., Managed Float (India) or Crawling Peg.

The Impossible Trinity: A country cannot simultaneously have Free capital movement, Fixed exchange rate, and Independent monetary policy.

Demand, Supply & Equilibrium

Law of Demand

Inverse relationship: As price increases, quantity demanded decreases. Downward sloping curve.

Exceptions: Giffen goods (inferior goods where demand rises as price rises), Veblen goods (luxury goods bought for status).

Determinants: Price, income, prices of related goods, tastes, expectations, population.

Law of Supply

Direct relationship: As price increases, quantity supplied increases. Upward sloping curve.

Determinants: Price, input costs, technology, taxes/subsidies, number of sellers.

Market Equilibrium

The point where Quantity Demanded equals Quantity Supplied. The wishes of buyers and sellers coincide.

  • Shortage (Excess Demand): Occurs when the price is below equilibrium. Pushes prices up.
  • Surplus (Excess Supply): Occurs when the price is above equilibrium. Pushes prices down.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 4 mins

Concept of Elasticity

Elasticity measures the responsiveness of a variable (like demand or supply) to a change in another variable (like price or income).

  • Price Elasticity of Demand (PED): % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price.
  • Income Elasticity (YED): % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Income. Normal Goods = Positive YED, Inferior Goods = Negative YED.
  • Cross Elasticity (XED): Responsiveness to the price of another good. Positive for substitutes, Negative for complements.
  • Point Elasticity: Measures elasticity at a finite point of the demand curve. Formula: Lower Segment / Upper Segment.
  • Arc Elasticity: Measures elasticity at the central point of an arc between two points.
  • Total Outlay Method: Analyzes total expenditure (Price x Quantity).

Consumer Utility & Surplus

Utility is the satisfaction, happiness, benefit or value a consumer gets from a good or service.

Cardinal Utility

Assumes satisfaction can be measured in exact, countable numbers called 'utils'.

Ordinal Utility

Assumes satisfaction cannot be scaled in numbers, but choices can be ranked by order of preference.

  • Total Utility (TU): Overall satisfaction derived from all units consumed.
  • Marginal Utility (MU): The amount of satisfaction gained by the consumption of one additional unit.
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility: As consumption increases, the marginal utility derived from each successive unit decreases consistently.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 3 mins

Indifference Curve & Budget Line

A curve connecting points representing different quantities of two goods, points between which a consumer is indifferent. Also called Iso-Utility Curve.

Properties:
  • Slopes downwards to the right.
  • Convex to the origin.
  • Indifference curves never intersect each other.

A graphical representation of all potential combinations of two commodities that can be bought given a specific income and prices.

Consumer Equilibrium: Achieved at the point where the Budget Line is exactly tangent to the highest attainable Indifference Curve. Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS) = Price Ratio (Px / Py).
⏱️ Estimated Read: 3 mins

Demand Forecasting

Forecasting means making an estimation in the present for a future occurring event to aid in tactical and strategic decision-making.

Methods of Demand Forecasting

  • Statistical Method: Using mathematical models and historical data.
  • Market Research / Surveying Method: Directly asking consumers about their buying intentions.
  • Sales Force Composite Method: Aggregating estimates from individual salespeople.
  • Delphi Method: A structured communication technique relying on a panel of experts.

Behavioral Economics & Segmentation

Economic theory assumes consumers are perfectly rational, but real life shows bounded rationality, emotional swings, and context-dependent preferences.

Behavioral Factors

  • Psychological: Motivation, perception, attitude.
  • Social: Family, peer influence, culture.
  • Emotional: Fear, joy, guilt.
  • Cognitive Biases: Anchoring, loss aversion.

Intro, Evolution & Objectives of HRM

What is HRM?
"The Planning, Organizing, Directing and Controlling of the Procurement, Development, Compensation, integration, maintenance and separation of human resources to the end that individual, organizational and societal objectives are accomplished." - Edward Filippo

Human Resource Management (HRM) is an operation designed to maximize employee performance in order to meet strategic goals. It is a continuous, pervasive, and people-oriented function.

The Evolution of HR

Timeline Era Focus Key Characteristics
1890s - 1913 Welfare Officer / Industrial Rev. Rise of factories. Workers seen as gears in a machine. Focus strictly on basic welfare and minimizing accidents.
1914 - 1939 Labour Manager / Scientific Mgmt Frederick Taylor's efficiency focus (Time & Motion studies). WWI/WWII required rapid recruitment and testing of soldiers.
1945 - 1979 Personnel Management Hawthorne Studies (Elton Mayo) proved psychological factors matter. HR was highly administrative, reactive, handling payroll and basic union disputes.
1980s - 1990s Human Resource Management Transition from purely administrative "personnel" to managing resources strategically. Focus on motivation and legal frameworks.
2000s - Present Strategic HRM / Human Capital Employees viewed as strategic assets providing a competitive advantage. HR aligns with business goals (HR Business Partner), driven by data and AI.

The 4 Primary Objectives of HRM

1. Personal Objectives: Assisting employees in achieving their personal goals (career growth, good pay) to ensure they are motivated and retained.
2. Functional Objectives: Maintaining the HR department's contribution at a level appropriate to the organization's needs. Resources are wasted if HR is overly complex for a small firm.
3. Organizational Objectives: Recognizing that HR is not an end in itself, but a means to help the company make a profit, expand, and achieve its primary business goals.
4. Societal Objectives: Operating ethically, ensuring compliance with labor laws, promoting diversity, and minimizing negative impacts on society.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 4 mins

Functions of HRM & Interlinkages

HRM functions are divided into three main categories: Managerial, Operative, and Advisory.

1. Managerial Functions

  • Planning: Preparing forecasts of future HR needs based on environment, mission, and culture.
  • Organizing: Grouping personnel activity logically into functions or positions; delegating authority.
  • Directing: Encouraging people to work willingly/effectively for the goals of the enterprise (Motivation & Leadership).
  • Controlling: Measuring progress towards goals, seeking planned results, and indicating corrective actions for deviations.

2. Operative Functions

  • Procurement: Job Analysis, Recruitment, Selection, Placement, Induction.
  • Development: Training, Executive Development, Career Planning, Organizational Development.
  • Compensation: Job evaluation, base compensation, incentives and benefits.
  • Maintenance/Integration: Health, safety, social security, industrial relations, dispute/grievance handling.

3. Advisory Functions

  • To Top Management: Advises on formulation of personnel programs, policies, and procedures.
  • To Department Heads: Offers advice on matters like manpower planning, job design, appraisals, and recruitment.
Interlinkage between HRM Functions: HR functions cannot operate in silos. For example, a poorly designed Job Analysis leads to bad Recruitment, which results in the need for excessive Training, which ultimately affects Performance Appraisals and causes high turnover. They represent a holistic employee lifecycle management system.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 3 mins

Digital Transformation in HRM

Moving from paper-based legacy systems to connected, data-driven HR processes. This shift transforms HR from an administrative burden to a strategic powerhouse.

1. HRIS (Human Resource Information Systems)

A centralized database holding all employee data (payroll, attendance, benefits). Eliminates data silos and reduces manual entry errors. Examples: Workday, SAP SuccessFactors.

2. Employee Self-Service (ESS)

Portals where employees can download payslips, apply for leave, or update tax info themselves without emailing HR. Saves thousands of HR hours annually.

3. Automation & AI

Automating repetitive tasks (like resume screening or onboarding emails) so HR professionals can focus on strategy, culture, and employee relations.

⏱️ Estimated Read: 4 mins

Crisis Management & Agile HR

Organizations face rapid disruptions where HR must lead people operations to maintain continuity. Did you know? 75.1% of organizations activated their crisis management team in just one year.

5 Types of Crises & HR Responsibilities

  • 1. Health and safety: Create emergency communication plans, from mass notifications to individual check-ins (e.g., COVID-19 policies).
  • 2. Workforce-related: Ensure transparent and timely communication around sensitive issues like layoffs, restructuring, or union strikes.
  • 3. Reputation & compliance: Take the lead in conducting thorough and impartial investigations into allegations of misconduct or PR scandals.
  • 4. Technology crises: Protect sensitive employee data (SSNs, banking) and manage access credentials during ransomware attacks or system compromises.
  • 5. Global/geopolitical: Provide relocation support/evacuation for staff and develop contingency plans to maintain operations across borders.

HRM in Agile and Hybrid Organizations

Agile HR describes a modern approach enabling organizations to be more responsive and innovative in the face of constant change.

Characteristics of Agile HR

  • Continuous Feedback: Scrapping annual reviews for weekly 1-on-1 check-ins.
  • Cross-functional Teams: Employees move fluidly between project squads instead of being stuck in one department.
  • Rapid Prototyping: HR tests new policies quickly rather than planning them for a year.

Challenges for HR

  • Maintaining culture and connection in hybrid (remote + office) setups.
  • Measuring productivity based on outcomes rather than hours logged.
  • Balancing strict labor compliance laws with the desire for extreme agility. Rethinking job leveling and career paths.

Employee Well-being, Burnout & Green HRM

Burnout is officially recognized by the WHO as an occupational phenomenon resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed.

The 6 Core Causes of Burnout

  • 1. Unsustainable Workload: Too much to do, too few resources.
  • 2. Perceived Lack of Control: Micromanagement, inability to influence decisions.
  • 3. Insufficient Rewards: Not just pay, but lack of recognition and prestige.
  • 4. Breakdown of Community: Toxic culture, unresolved conflicts, isolation.
  • 5. Absence of Fairness: Favoritism, unequal pay, unjust evaluations.
  • 6. Value Mismatch: Employee's deep personal values clash with corporate actions.

HR Interventions & Solutions

Offering mental health days without needing medical documentation. Providing Enhanced Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) for confidential holistic support (counseling, financial advising, legal aid).

Compressing the schedule. The 100-80-100 model means employees get 100% of their pay, work 80% of the time, and commit to maintaining 100% productivity.

Sustainability & Green HRM

Green HRM means using HR policies to promote the sustainable use of resources within organizations and support environmental protection.

ESG Alignment:
• Environmental: Carbon footprints, waste management, green policies.
• Social: Employee well-being, diversity, inclusion, and fair treatment.
• Governance: Board diversity, ethics, anti-corruption.
HR Function Green Practice & Corporate Example
Recruitment Patagonia: Seeks environmental activists. Emphasizes environmental responsibility in job postings, attracting over 900 applications per role. Offers 'Environmental Internship Programs' paying staff to work with nonprofits.
Onboarding & Training Unilever: Transitioned 80% of training to virtual platforms, saving an estimated 35,000 tons of CO2 annually. Developed 'sustainability champions' networks.
Performance & Rewards Microsoft: Embedded sustainability metrics into performance evaluations. Engineers are evaluated on code efficiency and energy optimization. Exec bonuses tied to ESG targets.

Intro to HR Analytics & Key Metrics

HR Analytics (People Analytics) involves gathering, analyzing, and reporting HR data to measure the impact of HR metrics on overall business performance. It enables data-driven decision-making rather than relying on intuition.

Crucial HR Metrics

Time to Hire / Time to Fill

The number of days between a job requisition opening and a candidate accepting the offer. A high number indicates a bottleneck in the recruitment pipeline.

Cost per Hire

Total recruitment costs (advertising, agency fees, recruiter salaries) divided by the number of hires. Helps optimize recruiting budgets.

Turnover Rate

The percentage of employees who leave the organization during a certain period. High turnover indicates poor culture, bad management, or uncompetitive pay.

eNPS (Employee Net Promoter Score)

Asks employees on a scale of 1-10: "How likely are you to recommend this company as a place to work?" Measures overall loyalty.

Absenteeism Rate

Measures the rate of unplanned absences. Spikes are an early warning sign of impending burnout or toxic management.

HR Dashboards: A business intelligence tool (like Tableau or PowerBI) that visually displays these metrics in real-time, allowing executives to quickly review critical KPIs like headcount, gender ratios, and training costs across locations.

Job Analysis & Design

Job Analysis involves systematically gathering and analyzing information about job roles and responsibilities. It is the absolute foundation for all HR activities—helping to develop descriptions, determine specifications, and set performance standards.

Define Objectives
Prepare JA
Perform JA
Design JD & JS
Control / Update

Job Description (JD)

A written document that explains the duties, responsibilities, and requirements of a job. (What the job is).

  • Job Role & Work Details
  • Requirements & Work Conditions
  • Employment Type

Job Specification (JS)

A list of recommended qualities, skills, traits, education, and experience a candidate needs to succeed. (Who fits the job).

  • Experience & education
  • Specific Technical Skills (e.g., Python)
  • Personality traits
Example: Data Scientist Position
JD details: "The Data Scientist will analyze large datasets, develop machine learning models, and collaborate with cross-functional teams to derive actionable insights."
JS details: "Master's or Ph.D. in Data Science, strong proficiency in Python and SQL, experience with machine learning algorithms, excellent communication skills."

Job Design & Re-engineering

If a job is boring or causing burnout, HR uses Re-engineering techniques to redesign it.

  • Job Rotation: Moving employees systematically from one job to another to reduce monotony.
  • Job Enlargement (Horizontal): Adding more tasks of the same level of difficulty to provide variety.
  • Job Enrichment (Vertical): Adding tasks that increase responsibility, autonomy, and control.
  • Job Simplification: Breaking a complex job down into small, highly specialized, repetitive tasks (Assembly line).

HR Planning & Forecasting

HRP is the process of forecasting the future human resource requirements of the organization and determining how to utilize existing capacity to meet these requirements.

The 5-Step HRP Process

1. Analyze Org Objectives
2. Inventory Current HR
3. Forecast Demand & Supply
4. Estimate Gaps
5. Formulate Action Plan
  • 1. Analyzing Organizational Objectives: If the company plans to open 5 new branches, HR needs to plan for expansion.
  • 2. Inventory of Current HR: Creating a skills inventory. What talents do we currently have? Who is retiring soon?
  • 3. Forecasting Demand and Supply: Using analytical models to predict exactly how many people we need (Demand) vs how many are available internally/externally (Supply).
  • 4. Estimating Gaps: Demand > Supply = Deficit (Need to hire). Demand < Supply = Surplus (Need to lay off or freeze hiring).
  • 5. Formulating Action Plan: Executing recruitment, training, or redundancy programs.

Workforce Forecasting Models

Quantitative Techniques (Data Driven)

  • Trend Analysis: Uses historical data to predict future workforce changes, including turnover rates, retirement patterns, and growth trajectories. E.g., analyzing that 5% retire and 6% resign annually.
  • Regression / Ratio-Trend Analysis: Statistical method linking workforce size to business variables such as sales growth, revenue targets, and market expansion.
  • Markov Analysis: Models internal employee movements like promotions, transfers, and exits using sophisticated transition probability matrices. Projects internal labor supply dynamics across time periods.

Qualitative Techniques (Judgment Driven)

  • Managerial Judgment: Department managers estimate their future needs based on their experience. Can be "Bottom-Up" or "Top-Down".
  • Delphi Technique: A structured communication technique where an anonymous panel of experts answers questionnaires in multiple rounds until consensus is reached, eliminating bias from loud voices.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 4 mins

Recruitment & Selection

Recruitment is the systematic process of identifying and attracting potential candidates to apply for job vacancies. It is a positive process (attracting as many candidates as possible), whereas Selection is a negative process (rejecting candidates until the best one remains).

The 6-Step Recruitment Process

  1. Identify Skills Gaps: Analyze current workforce strength and identify vacancies due to expansion, resignation, or new projects.
  2. Formulate JD & Advertise: Prepare the Job Description and broadcast vacancies through portals or newspapers.
  3. Review & Shortlist: Screen applications and eliminate unsuitable candidates.
  4. Interview & Test: Assess shortlisted candidates via HR/Technical panel interviews and aptitude/psychometric tests.
  5. Select Candidate: Make a final decision after reference checks and background verification.
  6. Make Job Offer: Roll out the formal offer letter including salary, benefits, and terms.

Internal Sources

Promotions, Transfers, Employee Referrals, Former employees.

Pros: Cheap, fast, boosts morale, known cultural fit, loyalty.

Cons: Prevents "new blood" and fresh ideas from entering the company, can cause conflict if one employee gets promoted over others.

External Sources

Campus Placements, Job Portals, Advertisements, Employment Exchanges.

Pros: Massive talent pool, brings in new ideas and already knowledgeable persons reducing training time.

Cons: Expensive, highly time-consuming, risk of bad cultural fit, can make existing employees unhappy about missed growth opportunities.

Scenario: The Campus Fest Crisis. A company needs 50 entry-level coders in one month. Internal transfers won't work (not enough volume). The best external source is Educational Institutions (Campus Recruitment) because it provides a massive, localized pool of fresh talent under one roof.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 3 mins

Performance Appraisal & Management

Performance Management is an ongoing, continuous process of communicating and clarifying job responsibilities, priorities, and performance expectations to ensure mutual understanding between manager and employee.

Performance Appraisal

The systematic evaluation of the performance of employees and to understand the abilities of a person for further growth and development.

  • Provides feedback.
  • Identifies training needs.
  • Forms the basis for promotions, incentives & salary hikes.

Modern Methods

  • MBO (Management by Objectives): Manager and employee jointly set specific, measurable goals.
  • BARS (Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales): Rates employees according to specific behaviors anchored to numerical ratings.

EVP Alignment & AI Ethics

EVP (Employee Value Proposition)

EVP is "what an employee gets in return for their skills & commitment". It includes compensation, career growth, work-life balance, and company culture.

AI helps align EVP by highlighting EVP keywords in JDs, matching candidate values with culture, and personalizing job messaging. Example: Matching a candidate seeking autonomy to a Startup, or one seeking stability to a Corporate.

Ethics & Bias in AI Hiring

"Is AI objective, or only as fair as its data?" AI learns from historical data. If past data is biased, the AI will be biased.

Amazon Case Study: Amazon scrapped a multi-million dollar AI resume screening tool because it learned to penalize resumes containing the word "women's" (e.g., "Women's Chess Club Captain"). Because Amazon's historical technical hires were predominantly male, the AI falsely correlated maleness with success.

Responsible AI Hiring Practices & GDPR

  • Human-in-the-loop: AI must support, not replace, final human judgment.
  • Regular Bias Audits: Constantly checking models for skewed outcomes regarding race, gender, or age.
  • Data Privacy (GDPR / DPDP Act, India): Candidates must explicitly consent to being evaluated by an AI, ensuring equal opportunity and fair employment practices.
  • Explainable AI: If an AI rejects a candidate, the company should ideally be able to explain why it made that decision, avoiding "black box" algorithms.

Financial Management: Intro & Objectives

Definition: Financial Management is the strategic planning, organizing, directing, and controlling of financial undertakings in an organization. According to Weston and Brigham, it harmonizes individual motives and enterprise goals.

The 3 Key Financial Decisions

1. Investment Decision (Capital Budgeting)

Selection of long-term assets in which funds will be invested by a firm (e.g., buying new machinery, launching a new product).

2. Financing Decision (Capital Structure)

Choosing the best mix of Debt (borrowed money) and Equity (owner's money) to finance the investments.

3. Dividend Decision

Determining what percentage of the profit should be distributed to shareholders as dividends vs. retained in the business for reinvestment.

Objectives & Role of Finance Manager

  • Profit Maximization: The traditional approach. Focuses purely on increasing the net profit of the firm in the short term. Ignores timing of returns and risk.
  • Wealth (Value) Maximization: The modern, superior approach. Focuses on increasing the net present value (NPV) and the market price of the company's shares over the long term. Considers the Time Value of Money and Risk.
  • Role of Manager: Forecasting capital requirements, raising funds, allocating funds into profitable ventures, and ensuring effective utilization.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 6 mins

Time Value of Money (TVM)

The core principle of finance: A rupee received today is worth more than a rupee received tomorrow due to its potential earning capacity (interest/inflation).

Finding the future value of a present sum. Interest is earned on principal + prior interest.

  • Lump Sum: $$FV = PV(1 + r)^n$$
  • Multiple Compounding Periods: $$FV = PV(1 + \frac{r}{m})^{m \times n}$$ (where m = times compounded per year).
  • Annuity (Regular equal payments): $$FVA = PMT \times \left[ \frac{(1 + r)^n - 1}{r} \right]$$

Finding the current worth of a future sum.

  • Lump Sum: $$PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}$$
  • Annuity: $$PVA = PMT \times \left[ \frac{1 - (1 + r)^{-n}}{r} \right]$$
  • Perpetuity (Infinite payments): $$PV = \frac{PMT}{r}$$
  • Effective Annual Rate (EAR): The actual interest rate earned due to compounding more than once a year. Formula: $$EAR = \left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^m - 1$$
  • Rule of 72: A quick shortcut to estimate how long it takes for an investment to double. Formula: $$Years = \frac{72}{\text{Interest Rate}}$$
  • Rule of 69: A more accurate shortcut for continuous compounding. Formula: $$Years = 0.35 + \frac{69}{\text{Interest Rate}}$$
⏱️ Estimated Read: 6 mins

Capital Budgeting Techniques

Techniques used to evaluate whether long-term investments are worth pursuing.

Technique Description & Formula Acceptance Rule
Payback Period (PBP) Time taken to recover the initial investment.
Formula: $$\frac{\text{Initial Inv}}{\text{Annual Cash Flow}}$$		 Accept if PBP < Target Period.
Net Present Value (NPV) Sum of PV of all future cash inflows minus Initial Investment.
Formula: $$\sum \frac{CF}{(1+r)^t} - C_0$$		 Accept if NPV > 0.
Internal Rate of Return (IRR) The discount rate that makes NPV equal to exactly zero. Accept if IRR > Cost of Capital.
Profitability Index (PI) Ratio of PV of Inflows to Initial Investment.
Formula: $$\frac{\text{PV of Inflows}}{\text{Initial Inv}}$$		 Accept if PI > 1.
⏱️ Estimated Read: 7 mins

Cost of Capital (WACC)

The minimum rate of return a firm must earn on its investments to maintain the market value of its shares. It is used as the discount rate in Capital Budgeting.

Cost of Debt ($K_d$)

Debt is usually the cheapest source of finance because interest payments are tax-deductible.

  • Irredeemable Debt: $$K_d = \frac{I(1 - t)}{NP}$$
  • Redeemable Debt: $$K_d = \frac{I(1 - t) + \frac{P - NP}{n}}{\frac{P + NP}{2}}$$

I = Interest, t = Tax Rate, NP = Net Proceeds, RV = Redemption Value, n = years.

Cost of Preference Shares ($K_p$)

Preference dividends are paid out of after-tax profits, so there is no tax adjustment.

  • Irredeemable: $$K_p = \frac{PD}{NP}$$
  • Redeemable: $$K_p = \frac{PD + \frac{RV - NP}{n}}{\frac{RV + NP}{2}}$$

PD = Preference Dividend.

Cost of Equity ($K_e$)

The return expected by ordinary shareholders. Highest risk = highest cost.

  • Dividend Yield Method: $$K_e = \frac{D}{NP}$$
  • Dividend Growth Model: $$K_e = \frac{D_1}{P_0} + g$$
  • Earnings Yield Method: $$K_e = \frac{EPS}{MP}$$
  • CAPM Approach: $$K_e = R_f + \beta(R_m - R_f)$$

Cost of Retained Earnings ($K_r$) & WACC

Retained Earnings: Opportunity cost of dividends forgone by shareholders. Usually $K_r = K_e$. If personal taxes/brokerage exist: $$K_r = K_e(1 - t_p)(1 - b)$$

WACC: The overall composite cost. Can be calculated using Book Value Weights or Market Value Weights.

$$WACC = \sum (W \times K)$$

⏱️ Estimated Read: 4 mins

Leverage (Operating & Financial)

Leverage refers to the use of fixed costs in an attempt to increase the potential return to shareholders.

Operating Leverage (DOL)

Arises from the presence of fixed operating costs (like rent, depreciation). It measures the sensitivity of EBIT to a change in Sales.

Formula: $$DOL = \frac{\text{Contribution}}{\text{EBIT}}$$

*Contribution = Sales - Variable Costs

Financial Leverage (DFL)

Arises from the presence of fixed financial charges (like Interest on debt). It measures the sensitivity of EPS to a change in EBIT.

Formula: $$DFL = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{EBT}}$$

Combined Leverage (DCL)

The total risk of the firm, showing the effect of a change in Sales on EPS.

Formula: $$DCL = DOL \times DFL = \frac{\text{Contribution}}{\text{EBT}}$$

⏱️ Estimated Read: 3 mins

Capital Structure Theories

Does changing the debt-to-equity ratio affect the value of the firm?

Relevant Theory: Yes, capital structure matters. Increasing debt (which is cheaper) decreases the overall cost of capital (WACC) and increases the value of the firm.

Irrelevant Theory: Capital structure does NOT matter. Any benefit from using cheaper debt is perfectly offset by equity shareholders demanding a higher return ($K_e$ rises) due to increased financial risk. WACC remains constant.

⏱️ Estimated Read: 3 mins

Working Capital Management

Management of the firm's short-term assets and liabilities to ensure continuous day-to-day operations.

  • Gross Working Capital: Total Current Assets (Inventory, Receivables, Cash).
  • Net Working Capital: Current Assets - Current Liabilities.
The Operating Cycle: The time duration from the purchase of raw materials to the realization of cash from debtors.
$$Operating Cycle = R + W + F + D - C$$
(Raw Material + Work in Progress + Finished Goods + Debtors - Creditors)

Exam Sums & Solutions 💯

A quick-glance guide with AT LEAST TWO solved examples for every single type of problem in the FM syllabus!

Problem A (Annual): Rohit deposits ₹30,000 at 7% compounded annually for 3 years. Find the maturity value.

Given: $PV = 30000$, $r = 0.07$, $n = 3$.

Solution:
$$FV = PV(1 + r)^n$$ $$FV = 30000(1.07)^3 = 30000 \times 1.2250 = \text{₹}36,751.3$$

Problem B (Semi-Annual): Kavya deposits ₹50,000 at 8% compounded semi-annually for 2 years. Find FV.

Given: $PV = 50000$, $r = 0.08 / 2 = 0.04$, $n = 2 \times 2 = 4$ periods.

Solution:
$$FV = 50000(1.04)^4 = 50000 \times 1.1698 = \text{₹}58,492.9$$

Problem A: Deposit ₹10,000 at the end of each year for 5 years at 10%. Find FVA.

Given: $PMT = 10000$, $r = 0.10$, $n = 5$.

Solution:
$$FVA = PMT \times \left[ \frac{(1+r)^n - 1}{r} \right]$$ $$FVA = 10000 \times \left[ \frac{(1.10)^5 - 1}{0.10} \right] = 10000 \times 6.1051 = \text{₹}61,051$$

Problem B: Deposit ₹5,000 at the end of each year for 4 years at 8%. Find FVA.

Solution:
$$FVA = 5000 \times \left[ \frac{(1.08)^4 - 1}{0.08} \right] = 5000 \times 4.5061 = \text{₹}22,530.5$$

Problem A: What is the present value of ₹1,00,000 to be received after 5 years at 10% discount rate?

Solution:
$$PV = \frac{FV}{(1+r)^n} = \frac{100000}{(1.10)^5} = 100000 \times 0.6209 = \text{₹}62,090$$

Problem B: What is the present value of ₹50,000 received after 3 years at 12%?

Solution:
$$PV = \frac{50000}{(1.12)^3} = 50000 \times 0.7118 = \text{₹}35,590$$

Problem A: Receive ₹10,000 annually for 5 years at 10%. Find PVA.

Solution:
$$PVA = PMT \times \left[ \frac{1 - (1 + r)^{-n}}{r} \right]$$ $$PVA = 10000 \times \left[ \frac{1 - (1.10)^{-5}}{0.10} \right] = 10000 \times 3.7908 = \text{₹}37,908$$

Problem B: Receive ₹20,000 annually for 4 years at 8%. Find PVA.

Solution:
$$PVA = 20000 \times \left[ \frac{1 - (1.08)^{-4}}{0.08} \right] = 20000 \times 3.3121 = \text{₹}66,242$$

Problem A (Rule of 72): How long will it take for money to double at an 8% interest rate?

Solution:
$$\text{Years} = \frac{72}{\text{Interest Rate}} = \frac{72}{8} = \mathbf{9} \text{ Years}$$

Problem B (Rule of 69): Calculate the exact doubling time for continuous compounding at 8%.

Solution:
$$\text{Years} = 0.35 + \frac{69}{8} = 0.35 + 8.625 = \mathbf{8.975} \text{ Years}$$

Problem A (Irredeemable): Company issues ₹1,00,000 10% debentures at par. Tax is 30%.

Solution:
$$K_d = \frac{I(1 - t)}{NP} = \frac{10000(1 - 0.30)}{100000} = \frac{7000}{100000} = \mathbf{7\%}$$

Problem B (Redeemable): 1,000 10% debentures of ₹100 issued at 10% premium, redeemable at par in 5 years. Tax 30%.

Given: I=10, NP=110, RV=100, n=5, t=0.30.

Solution:
$$Numerator = 10(1 - 0.30) + \frac{100 - 110}{5} = 7 - 2 = 5$$
$$Denominator = \frac{100 + 110}{2} = 105$$
$$K_d = \frac{5}{105} \times 100 = \mathbf{4.76\%}$$

Problem A (Irredeemable): ₹1,00,000 12% pref shares issued at a 5% discount.

Given: PD = 12000, NP = 95000.

Solution:
$$K_p = \frac{12000}{95000} \times 100 = \mathbf{12.63\%}$$

Problem B (Redeemable): ₹100 pref shares issued at par, 10% dividend, redeemable at 5% premium after 5 years. Flotation cost 2%.

Given: PD=10, NP=98 (100-2), RV=105, n=5.

Solution:
$$K_p = \frac{10 + \frac{105 - 98}{5}}{\frac{105 + 98}{2}} = \frac{10 + 1.4}{101.5} = \frac{11.4}{101.5} = \mathbf{11.23\%}$$

Problem A (CAPM): Risk-free rate is 6%, market return is 12%, Beta is 1.2.

Solution:
$$K_e = R_f + \beta(R_m - R_f) = 6 + 1.2(12 - 6) = 6 + 7.2 = \mathbf{13.2\%}$$

Problem B (Dividend Growth): Expected dividend (D1) is ₹2, Current Market Price (P0) is ₹20, Growth rate (g) is 5%.

Solution:
$$K_e = \frac{D_1}{P_0} + g = \left(\frac{2}{20}\right) + 0.05 = 0.10 + 0.05 = \mathbf{15\%}$$

Problem A (With Personal Tax): Ke is 15%. Personal tax rate is 20%. Brokerage cost is 2%.

Solution:
$$K_r = K_e(1 - t_p)(1 - b) = 15(1 - 0.20)(1 - 0.02)$$ $$K_r = 15(0.80)(0.98) = \mathbf{11.76\%}$$

Problem B (Without Personal Tax): If no personal tax or brokerage is mentioned, and Ke is 12%.

Solution:
$$K_r = K_e = \mathbf{12\%}$$
