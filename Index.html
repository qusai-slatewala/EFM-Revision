Introduction to Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics studies the behavior of the whole economy (national and international). It originated after the Great Depression of 1929 when classical theory (which assumed there would always be full employment) failed to explain massive unemployment (25% in the USA). John Maynard Keynes revolutionized the field in 1936.

Scope of Macroeconomics: Economic Growth, Theory of National Income, Money, International Trade, Employment, General Price Level, Fiscal Policy, and Monetary Policy.

Circular Flow in a Four-Sector Economy

The economy consists of four interacting sectors through which money and resources flow:

1. Household Sector

Provides factors of production (land, labor, capital) to firms and consumes goods and services.

2. Firms Sector

Produces goods/services for the market and makes factor payments (wages, rent) to households.

3. Government Sector

Regulates the economy, collects revenue (taxes), and spends on public services.

4. Foreign Sector

Handles transactions with the rest of the world (Exports minus Imports).

Leakages vs. Injections

  • Leakages: Outflow of income from the circular flow (Savings, Taxes, Imports).
  • Injections: Inflow of income into the circular flow (Investment, Government Expenditure, Exports).
  • Equilibrium condition: Leakages = Injections.

National Income Concepts

National income is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced by a nation during a period of time. Formula: Y = C + I + G + (X - M)

  • To compute and compare the economic advancement of a country.
  • To measure the contribution of different sectors to the country's economic growth.
  • To assist the government in planning and implementing various projects.
  • To determine the limitations and benefits of various economic activities like production, consumption, and distribution.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP): The standard measure of the value added created through the production of goods and services in a country. Excludes intermediate goods to avoid double counting.

Net National Product (NNP): The market value of all finished goods produced by citizens (domestic and overseas) minus depreciation. NNP = GNP - Depreciation.

  1. Income Method: Measured at distribution level (Compensation + Rent + Interest + Profit + Mixed Income + Net factor income from abroad).
  2. Product/Value Added Method: Measured at production level. Gross national product minus Cost of Capital minus Depreciation minus Indirect Taxes.
  3. Expenditure Method: Measured at spending level (Consumption + Investment + Govt Spending + Net Exports).
  • Personal Income (PI): All earnings made by a household (salary, wages, dividends, rent). Equation: Private income - Corporate profit tax - Undistributed profits by corporations + Transfers by corporations and government to households.
  • Disposable Income (DPI): Money available for consumption/saving after paying personal income taxes (DPI = PI - Taxes).
  • Discretionary Income: The amount of net income remaining after all necessities (shelter, food, debts) are covered.
  • Per Capita Income (PCI): Average income earned per person (National Income / Population). Used to evaluate standard of living.
The Poverty Premium: Individuals with lower incomes often face additional costs to access essential goods and services. Key contributing factors include:

1. Limited Access to Affordable Credit.
2. Higher Costs for Everyday Goods and Services.
3. Insufficient Access to Banking Services.
4. Limited Options for Housing.
5. Higher Costs for Utilities.
6. Transportation Challenges.

Journey of the Indian Economy

India adopted a mixed economy model post-independence (1947) to counter the impoverishment inflicted by British deindustrialization. The nation's share of world income had drastically fallen from 22.6% in 1700 to 3.8% by 1952.

  • 1st Five-Year Plan (1951): Harrod-Domar model focused on agriculture and irrigation.
  • 2nd Five-Year Plan (1956): Mahalanobis model focused on rapid heavy industrialization (Licence Raj era).
  • Lal Bahadur Shastri: Focus on food security, initiation of the Green Revolution, and Operation Flood (milk).
  • Industrial Establishments: Setup of Bhilai, Rourkela, and Durgapur steel plants with foreign assistance.
  • Indira Gandhi: 'Garibi Hatao' slogan. Nationalization of 14 major private banks in 1969. FERA (1973) enacted to control foreign investments. Established the Food Corporation of India (FCI) in 1965.
  • 1980s (Rajiv Gandhi): Early deregulation, reduction in import duties, and ushering in the IT & telecom revolutions. Establishment of NABARD (1982).
  • Chakravarty Committee (1982): Recommended an acceptable inflation rate of 4% per year.
  • Other 1970s and 1980s Events: Discovery of Mumbai High oil field (1974), 1977 Elections (Janata Party), Launch of Maruti Car (1983), Bhopal Gas Tragedy highlighting industrial risks (1984), and Formation of SAARC (1985).
  • 1991 Crisis: High fiscal deficit, low forex reserves (enough for only 2 weeks of imports), high inflation.
  • Reforms (Narasimha Rao & Manmohan Singh): Liberalisation (ending Licence Raj), Privatisation (disinvestment), and Globalisation (unrestricted cross-border movement).
  • 1992 Harshad Mehta Scam: Led to the rise of SEBI.
  • Late 1990s: Establishment of Rural Infrastructure Development Fund (1995-96) and Kisan Credit Card (1998) to assist rural and agricultural credit. Formation of the Disinvestment Commission (1996), 73rd Amendment Act (Panchayati Raj in 1992), and Formation of G-20 (1999).
  • 2000s: SEZ Policy, FRBM Act, NREGA (MGNREGS). High economic growth under Manmohan Singh.
  • Early 2000s Initiatives: Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) formed in 2000; Golden Quadrilateral Project launched in 2001. Ministry of Disinvestment (2001); Dismantling of Administered Pricing Mechanism (APM) in 2002.
  • Late 2000s Initiatives: MSME Development Act (2006); National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC) launched in 2008.
  • 2010s: Skill India, Smart Cities, Demonetisation (2016), GST Implementation (2017).
  • 2010s Programs: National Food Security Act (2013), Deen Dayal Upadhyaya Grameen Kaushalya Yojana (DDU-GKY) in 2014, MUDRA Bank (2015), Atal Innovation Mission (2016), and National Civil Aviation Policy (2016).
  • 2020s: COVID-19 lockdown impact, Migrant crisis, Labour Codes (2020), PM Cares Fund.

Government Budget & Macro Policies

Government Budget: Divided into Revenue Budget (Tax/Non-Tax revenue, recurrent expenditures) and Capital Budget (Capital receipts, capital expenditure). Objectives include resource reallocation, reducing inequalities, economic stability, and growth.

Fiscal Policy vs. Monetary Policy

Feature Fiscal Policy Monetary Policy
Definition Government use of spending and taxation to influence the economy. Central bank control of money supply and interest rates to promote growth.
Controlled By Government. Central Bank.
Major Tools Public Expenditure, Taxation, Public Borrowing. Bank Rate, CRR, SLR, Repo Rate, Open Market Operations (OMOs).

Fiscal Policy Objectives

  • Economic Growth: Maintain growth rate to achieve economic goals.
  • Price Stability: Control price levels when inflation is too high.
  • Full Employment: Achieve near full employment to recover from low economic activity.

Monetary Tools Explained

  • CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio): % of deposits banks must keep as cash with RBI.
  • SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio): % of deposits banks must hold in safe assets (gold, govt securities).
  • Repo Rate: Interest rate at which RBI lends short-term money to banks.
  • Reverse Repo Rate: Rate at which RBI absorbs liquidity from banks.

Types of Monetary Policy:

  • Expansionary: Helps speed up the economy and increase economic growth.
  • Contractionary: Helps slow down the economy and control growth/inflation.

Business Cycle, Unemployment, and Inflation

The Business Cycle

The alternate expansion and contraction in overall business activity. Stages: Expansion → Peak → Contraction → Trough.

Features of Business Cycle:

  • Recurrent in nature.
  • Do not occur at regular intervals.
  • They are synchronic.
  • Self-generating (Forces of one phase lead to forces of another phase).

Causes:

  • Internal Factors: Fluctuations in Demand, Fluctuations in Investment, Variations in government spending.
  • External Factors: Wars, Post War Reconstruction, Technology Shocks, Natural Factors.

Types of Unemployment

Frictional

Short-term; transitioning between jobs or looking for new employment.

Structural

Longer-lasting; caused by fundamental technological shifts in the economy.

Cyclical

Resulting directly from economic downturns in the business cycle.

Disguised

When workers are redundant; their marginal productivity is essentially zero (common in agriculture).

Seasonal

Unemployment that occurs during certain seasons of the year when demand for labor is lower.

Inflation

The general, sustained increase in prices, resulting in a decrease in the purchasing power of money. According to Coulborn, inflation is defined as "too much money chasing too few goods."

  • Causes: Money Supply Increases, Aggregate Demand Increases (Demand-Pull), Aggregate Supply Decreases (Cost-Push).
  • Types based on rate: Creeping (<3%), Chronic/Secular (persists for a long period), Walking (3-10%), Running (>10%), Galloping (>20%), Hyperinflation (>1000%).
  • Effects: Reduces Purchasing Power, Lowers Savings, Increase in cost of living, Decreased Investment.
  • Control Measures: Monetary policy (Credit Control), Fiscal policy (Reduction in Unnecessary Expenditure, Increase in Taxes), Price Control.
  • Stagflation: High inflation + Economic stagnation (High unemployment).
  • Shrinkflation: Shrinking a product's quantity/volume while keeping the packaging and price the same.

Other Categorizations of Inflation

  • By Coverage: Comprehensive (Economy-wide) vs. Sporadic inflation.
  • By Government Reaction: Open inflation vs. Suppressed/Repressed inflation.
  • By Expectation: Anticipated/Expected vs. Unanticipated/Unexpected inflation.

Multiplier, Trade & Exchange Rates

Developed by Keynes during the Great Depression. It explains how an initial change in spending (e.g., government spending) leads to a more than proportional change in national income. One person's spending becomes another's income.

Influenced by: Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC). A higher MPC leads to a larger multiplier effect.

Other Types of Multipliers: Investment Multiplier, Tax Multiplier, Government Spending Multiplier, Money Supply Multiplier, and Deposit Multiplier.

The difference between the value of a country's exports and its imports over a period. It is the largest component of the Balance of Payments (BOP).

  • Trade Surplus: Exports > Imports.
  • Trade Deficit: Imports > Exports.
  • Fixed (Pegged): Currency tied to another major currency (e.g., USD). Curbs inflation but limits monetary policy flexibility.
  • Flexible (Floating): Value determined by market forces (supply/demand). Greater flexibility but can be volatile.
  • Intermediate (Hybrid): E.g., Managed Float (India) or Crawling Peg.

The Impossible Trinity: A country cannot simultaneously have Free capital movement, Fixed exchange rate, and Independent monetary policy.

Currency Fluctuations: Caused by Interest Rates, Inflation, Political Stability, and Trade Balance. These fluctuations directly impact business import costs, consumer prices, and investment returns.

Current Macroeconomic Challenges

Recent global and domestic events pose significant challenges to economic stability and growth.

  • Sluggish Global Recovery: Uneven post-pandemic rebound; China’s slowdown affecting global demand.
  • Geopolitical Flashpoints: Indo-Pak tensions, Ukraine, and Middle East conflicts are straining supply chains and investor confidence.
  • Persistent Inflation: Food, fuel, and services remain costly despite central bank tightening.
  • Tariff Impacts: Tariffs on exports (e.g., 27% reciprocal tariffs on Indian steel/aluminium) reduce export competitiveness.
  • Policy Dilemma: Central banks must balance inflation control with preserving economic growth.
  • China's Competitive Edge: Aggressive expansion in AI, EVs, and global supply chains puts pressure on other nations to boost manufacturing and innovation.
  • Employment & Inequality: Youth unemployment and income disparity remain pressing issues, reshaped by automation and global shocks.

Definition: Reducing fiscal deficit and public debt to ensure long-term economic stability.

Progress: Fiscal deficit reduced from 9.2% of GDP (FY21) to 5.6% (FY24), targeting 4.9% in FY25.

Key Tools: Rationalizing subsidies, boosting tax compliance, and strategic disinvestment.

  • Impact on Importers: As a major oil importer, price swings directly affect India's inflation, trade deficit, and rupee value.
  • Sectoral Impact: Refiners face margin pressures, while paints, cement, and auto sectors see rising input costs.

Demand, Supply & Equilibrium

Demand & Supply Schedules: A tabular representation of the list of quantities of a commodity demanded or supplied at different price levels during a certain period of time. Market supply is the total sum of the supply amounts for all individual producers.

Law of Demand

Inverse relationship: As price increases, quantity demanded decreases. Downward sloping curve.

Determinants: Price, income, prices of related goods, tastes, expectations, population.

Law of Supply

Direct relationship: As price increases, quantity supplied increases. Upward sloping curve.

Determinants: Price, input costs, technology, taxes/subsidies, number of sellers, firm's business objective, and nature of competition.

Market Equilibrium

[Image of supply and demand market equilibrium graph]

The point where Quantity Demanded equals Quantity Supplied. The wishes of buyers and sellers coincide.

  • Shortage (Excess Demand): Occurs when the price is below equilibrium. Pushes prices up.
  • Surplus (Excess Supply): Occurs when the price is above equilibrium. Pushes prices down.

Shifts vs. Movements

Type Cause Effect on Curve
Movement (Extension/Contraction) Change in the product's own price. Moving up or down along the same curve.
Shift (Increase/Decrease) Change in other determinants (Income, Tech, etc.). Entire curve shifts Left (Decrease) or Right (Increase).

Concept of Elasticity

Elasticity measures the responsiveness of a variable (like demand or supply) to a change in another variable (like price or income).

  • Price Elasticity of Demand (PED): % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price.
  • Income Elasticity (YED): % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Income.
  • Cross Elasticity (XED): Responsiveness to the price of another good. Positive for substitutes, Negative for complements.
  • Perfectly Elastic: Horizontal curve.
  • Relatively Elastic: Flatter curve (Elasticity is between -1 and -∞).
  • Unitary Elastic: Elasticity is exactly -1.
  • Relatively Inelastic: Steeper curve (Elasticity is between 0 and -1).
  • Perfectly Inelastic: Vertical curve.
  • Point Elasticity: Measures elasticity at a finite point of the demand curve. Formula: Lower Segment / Upper Segment.
  • Arc Elasticity: Measures elasticity at the central point of an arc between a pair of two points.
  • Total Outlay Method: Analyzes total expenditure (Price x Quantity).
    - If outlay remains the same with price change: Unitary elastic (e=1).
    - If outlay decreases with a price rise: Relatively elastic (e>1).
    - If outlay increases with a price rise: Relatively inelastic (e<1).
  • Product Pricing: Choosing between Price Skimming vs. Penetration Pricing based on elasticity.
  • Revenue Forecasting: Highly elastic products have volatile revenue streams.
  • Promotion Planning: Discounts and sales are far more effective for elastic goods.
  • Example: Airline tickets (highly elastic) require dynamic pricing, whereas insulin (highly inelastic) maintains steady revenue regardless of price.

Consumer Utility & Surplus

Utility is the satisfaction or value a consumer gets from a good or service.

Cardinal Utility

Assumes satisfaction can be measured in exact, countable numbers called 'utils'.

Ordinal Utility

Assumes satisfaction cannot be measured in numbers, but choices can be ranked by order of preference.

Key Utility Concepts

  • Total Utility (TU): Overall satisfaction derived from all units consumed.
  • Marginal Utility (MU): Additional satisfaction from consuming one additional unit.
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility (DMU): As consumption increases, the marginal utility derived from each successive unit decreases.
  • Equimarginal Principle: Consumers maximize total utility when the ratio of marginal utility to price is equal across all goods (MUa/Pa = MUb/Pb).
Consumer Surplus: The economic measure of a buyer's excess benefit. It is the difference between the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay and the actual market price they pay. Area below the demand curve and above the price line.

Indifference Curve & Budget Line

Ordinal utility uses indifference curves and budget lines to determine consumer equilibrium.

A curve connecting points representing combinations of two goods that give the consumer equal levels of satisfaction.

Indifference Map: A set of Indifference Curves, where a higher IC indicates a higher level of satisfaction.

Properties:
  • Slopes downwards to the right.
  • Convex to the origin.
  • Indifference curves never intersect each other.
  • A higher IC indicates a higher level of satisfaction.

A graphical representation of all potential combinations of two commodities that can be bought given a specific income and prices. Also called the Price Line.

Budget Set: The set of all possible combinations of the two commodities a consumer can afford to buy with their given income and market prices.

Equation: M = (Px * Qx) + (Py * Qy)

Consumer Equilibrium: Achieved at the point where the Budget Line is exactly tangent to the highest attainable Indifference Curve. Here, the consumer maximizes satisfaction within their budget constraints.

Demand Forecasting

Estimating future demand based on present data to aid in tactical and strategic decision-making.

Based on Economy

  • Macro-level: General economic environment (National Income, IIP).
  • Industry-level: Demand for an entire industry (e.g., all cement in India).
  • Firm-level: Demand for a specific brand (e.g., Birla Cement).

Based on Time Period

  • Short-term: Usually 6 months to 1 year. For tactical decisions.
  • Long-term: 2 to 5+ years. For strategic decisions like plant expansion.

Methods of Demand Forecasting

Statistical Methods, Market Research/Surveys, Sales Force Composite, Delphi Method, Barometric Method, and A/B Experimentation.

Behavioral Economics & Segmentation

Economic theory assumes consumers are perfectly rational utility maximizers, but real life shows bounded rationality, emotional swings, and context-dependent preferences.

Economic Rationality (Traditional)

  • Consumers are perfectly rational.
  • Consumers have full information.
  • Preferences are stable.
  • Utility is consistent.

Behavioral Realism (Reality)

  • Often bounded rationality.
  • Information asymmetry.
  • Context-dependent preferences.
  • Emotional swings affect decisions.

Behavioral Factors

  • Psychological: Motivation, perception, attitude. (Influences branding tone) .
  • Social: Peer influence, culture.
  • Emotional: Fear, joy, guilt.
  • Cognitive Biases: Anchoring, loss aversion. (Affects pricing & offers) .
  • Heuristics: Rule-of-thumb decision making. (Shapes store layout) .
Application in Segmentation: By combining behavioral insights with economic elasticity, marketers can effectively segment audiences. For example:
- Price-Sensitive segment: Highly elastic. Strategy: Discounts, bundling.
- Brand-Loyal segment: Inelastic. Strategy: Premium pricing.
- Trend-Followers: Medium elasticity. Strategy: Fast turnover, influencer marketing.
- Functional Buyers: Practical use. Varies elasticity. Strategy: Feature-led marketing.

Elasticity Application Examples

Product / Segment Elasticity Profile Pricing / Marketing Strategy
Fashion Retail Highly Elastic Lower prices to boost revenue (Discounts).
Petrol & Medicines Highly Inelastic Increase prices for higher revenue.
Smartphones High Cross Elasticity, Positive Income Elasticity Target upper-middle class.
Instant Noodles Low Cross Elasticity, Negative Income Elasticity Target lower-income groups.
